The problem

Every fitness app gives you
a routine. None of them know
what you actually trained.

"I trained chest and shoulders today. Every app gave me the same generic upper body stretch — half of it was for muscles I never touched."

The problem isn't a lack of stretch content. There's plenty of that. The problem is that stretch recommendations are disconnected from training sessions. Most apps are content libraries with a search bar — they expect you to know what you need and go find it.

There's also a sequencing problem almost nobody addresses. Static stretching before training reduces force output. The correct order is activation → dynamic → static. Most apps either don't know this or don't care — they'll happily show you a hamstring hold before your squat session.

The solution isn't more content. It's a decision engine — something that takes your session as input and produces a correctly-ordered, non-redundant plan as output.

Value propositions

Five things
that earn their place.

Every product decision traces back to one of these.

01 — Core
Muscle-first planning

Start with what you trained today. The plan is different every time because your training is. Not a routine — a response.

02 — Safety
Sequence enforced by design

Activation → Dynamic → Static. Always. It's structurally impossible to stretch in the wrong order. The product doesn't trust the user to remember — it handles it.

03 — Efficiency
Every exercise earns its place

A greedy coverage algorithm picks the highest-coverage exercises first. Quick mode = minimum effective dose. Optimised = best return on time. No filler, ever.

04 — Friction
Zero friction, zero account

Open browser → tap muscles → get plan. Under 10 seconds. No sign-up, no app store, no subscription. Works offline. Plans saved locally.

05 — Understanding
The why, not just the what

Every exercise has the biomechanical reason it's there — specific to the muscles you trained, not generic advice. Plus a pro tip: the cue most people miss. You understand what you're doing, not just follow instructions.

Technical decisions

Every tradeoff
was deliberate.

These are the decisions that shaped the architecture — and the reasons behind them.

Architecture
No backend. No database. No server.

The entire app — 132 exercises, plan logic, save/load, PWA — runs in a single HTML file. All data is embedded at build time. Plans persist in localStorage.

Tradeoff accepted: No user accounts, no cross-device sync, no analytics. Worth it for zero infrastructure cost, zero latency, and offline-first capability.
Algorithm
Greedy coverage selection, not ranking

The selection algorithm is a two-pass greedy: first pass picks exercises by unique muscle coverage descending, second pass fills remaining budget from the pool. This ensures no muscle is left uncovered and no exercise is redundant.

Tradeoff accepted: Deterministic first generation (best plan always shown first). Randomisation only on explicit Refresh tap — users get variety on demand, not randomness by default.
Data model
Muscle group as the primary key

Every exercise is tagged to muscle groups, not body regions or equipment. Selection, filtering, coverage calculation, and display all resolve through muscle group — making the whole system consistent and extensible.

Tradeoff accepted: More complex tagging upfront. Paid off in clean algorithm logic and zero special-casing across 132 exercises.
Depth modes
Three modes, same algorithm, different caps

Quick, Optimised, and Detailed are not different algorithms — they're the same greedy selection with dynamic caps that scale with the number of muscles selected. Single muscle = tighter cap, full body = wider cap.

Tradeoff accepted: More cognitive load in explaining mode differences. Worth it because the underlying consistency makes the system predictable and the output always valid.
Deployment
GitHub → Cloudflare Pages, zero config

Static files in a GitHub repo. Cloudflare Pages deploys on every commit in under 30 seconds. Custom domain via DNS CNAME. SSL automatic. No build step, no CI/CD pipeline to maintain.

Tradeoff accepted: No server-side rendering, no dynamic routes. Not needed for this use case — and the simplicity makes iteration fast.
Product journey

From frustration
to production.

Six phases, no team, no budget. What each phase taught me.

Phase 01 — Problem definition
The insight that started everything

The observation was simple: I'd just finished a heavy chest session and couldn't find a single app that would tell me what to stretch based on what I actually trained. Everything was either generic or required me to already know the answer. That's a product gap, not a content gap.

PM insight: The best product ideas come from noticing that you're doing manual work a computer should be doing.
Phase 02 — Architecture
Deciding what the product would never do

Before writing a line of code, I made a list of things FlexFlow would never do: require an account, need a server, store data in the cloud, show ads, or recommend exercises that weren't specific to what you trained. Constraints clarify everything. Every subsequent decision was easier because of this list.

PM insight: A clear scope of what you won't build is as important as what you will. Constraints are strategy.
Phase 03 — Database
132 exercises, 3 phases, 17 muscle groups

Building the exercise database was the most time-intensive part — not technically, but intellectually. Every exercise needed to be tagged, phased, prioritised, and written with instructions and a biomechanical rationale. Getting the activation phase right (the overlooked middle between warm-up and stretching) was the hardest — there was almost no reference material for it.

PM insight: Content quality is a product decision. Bad data makes even a good algorithm useless.
Phase 04 — Core logic
The algorithm that makes the product work

The first version of the selection algorithm was too simple — it just sorted by coverage and took the top N. It worked but produced the same plan every time. The second iteration added a fill pass for single-muscle selections (which were getting too few exercises) and a Refresh feature that shuffles the pool before selection — giving variety without sacrificing coverage guarantees.

PM insight: "Works" and "works well" are different bars. The first version shipped. The second version earned trust.
Phase 05 — Feedback loops
Six rounds of visual and UX iteration

The product went through six+ rounds of changes driven by real usage observations: the muscle list was too small on mobile, the gold activation colour wasn't visible in light mode, the expanded card showed a white background in dark mode, the Refresh button wasn't findable, SVG highlights weren't contrasty enough. None of these were bugs — they were gaps between what was built and what worked in practice.

PM insight: The gap between "technically correct" and "actually usable" only shows up when someone real uses it.
Phase 06 — Production
Deployed, live, and iterable

FlexFlow is live at a custom domain, deployed via Cloudflare Pages from a GitHub repo. Any change to the exercise database, algorithm, or UI is live in under 30 seconds. The PWA installs to home screen on both iOS and Android. The architecture that seemed over-engineered at the start turned out to be exactly right — it made the whole iteration cycle fast and cheap.

PM insight: Deployment speed is a product feature. Fast iteration is a competitive advantage even when you're a team of one.

What it proved

Full cycle ownership is learnable. Idea → architecture → database → algorithm → UI → feedback → deployment. Going through every phase solo, without a team to delegate to, forces understanding of the whole system.

Constraints produce better products. No backend, no account, no filler — every constraint forced a cleaner decision. The product is better because of what it doesn't do.

The thesis holds. Fitness tech is under-designed and over-engineered. A single person, in weeks, can ship something more useful than products with full teams behind them — if the product thinking is right.
